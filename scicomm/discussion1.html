Creating Your Own Science Communication Project

Now that youve started thinking critically about science communication, we are going to pivot the way discussion sections will work through the end of the semester. We are now entering the creative discovery project. Over the next 5 weeks, you will be working in small groups to create your own piece of science communication!

More specifically:

  1. You will learn how to access and read primary scientific literature in order to find scientific information that you want to communicate to a non-scientific audience. The topic you will cover for this exercise is: genetic influences on skin pigmentation. Your TF will lead you to the articles to use and help you pull out the salient information.

  2. You will work with 2-3 other students in your discussion section to develop a small SciComm project, such as a TikTok video, a Twitter thread, Instagram posts, a short Adobe Spark presentation, or science-themed webcomic, etc. This is not meant to be a huge project. This is a small SciComm project aimed at being scientifically accurate, entertaining/captivating, and accessible.

  3. You will have time in discussion sections to work on this, but should also plan to spend 2-3 hours outside of section working with your group.

  4. You will learn how to provide productive peer-review for your classmates and how to incorporate the peer-review of others into your own project.

  5. You will be assessed, in part, on the process of creating your SciComm piece and on helping other groups improve their projects.

    • If you are enrolled in CAS AN/WS 233, the process and the final product will be worth 100 points.

  6. You will write a reflective essay on the process.

    • If you are enrolled in CAS AN/WS 233, this essay will be a component of your final exam (you may write this before the final and bring it to class as part of your notes).

Part 1: Science Communication

In this section, you should discuss with your peers the various forms of science communication.

Coming into discussion today, youve (hopefully) completed an assignment that asked you to analyze a few pieces of science communication, considering any strengths and drawbacks you may have noticed.

Some points to consider:

  • What are the different types of science communication we encounter every day?
  • How does the general public make sense of and participate in scientific discussion?
  • What role do different forms of science communication have?
  • What have we learned about how science communication can build up or break down trust in science for the general public?

What about scientific literature, such as academic journal articles?

Some helpful terms to know:

Journal Article: A report of recent research or a review compiling research, written by those conducting or otherwise overseeing research in a given field of study. These articles undergo a peer-review process. Also known as research literature, research article, research paper, or a journal article; journal articles can be primary research or a review of previous research.

Peer-Review: A process by which research is reviewed by a group of experts in a given field, usually two or three people, managed by a peer-review editor who oversees the peer-review process. Based on peer-review, a journal article may be accepted for publication, provisionally accepted with minor revisions, provisionally accepted with major revisions, or rejected for publication.

Reliable sources include:

  • Peer-reviewed academic journal article describing original research in science
  • Peer-reviewed academic journal article compiling other science research into a primary review article
  • Why are these reliable?

What is not a reliable source:

  • Journal that is not peer-reviewed
  • Journal that has many articles written by members of the editorial board
  • Why are these unreliable?

What is a non-primary source? Have we seen or discussed some examples in this class already? How do we know?
